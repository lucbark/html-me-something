-Joe Turner

Meditations:

The personal observations and reflections of Marcus Aurelius. The last of the "Five Good Emperors" may have been writing to himself but his collection of aphorisms and insights on autonomy and judgement is an essential work in philosophy. He was the emperor of Rome but one cannot learn that from his entries. Marcus focuses on being a fellow human. If Kallipolis had a leader it would be Marcus Aurelius.

The Demon-Haunted World:

Carl Sagan provided us with a manifesto on how to deal with unreason. Unfortunately, 20 years have passed and people still cling to charlatans and fictional narratives for their answers. It may no longer be hip to believe in psychics and martians but for whatever reason purchasing "power crystals" and avoiding a vitamin K shot is all the rage. Fortunately, Sagan's advice is durable and works on even the most recent additions to the pseudoscience Rolodex.Informed decisions are possible with observations, measurements, experiments, and testable hypotheses. If we are going to see through falsehoods it will be with the light of science. Carl Sagan was our Prometheus and he gave us fire. "All science asks is to employ the same levels of skepticism we use in buying a used car or in judging the quality of analgesics or beer from their television commercials." -Carl Sagan

Kafka on the Shore:

Metaphysics. I’ll tell you what... Murakami’s novel isn’t finished even after you have read the last page. “Memories warm you up from the inside. But they also tear you apart.”

Hard-Boiled Wonderland and the End of the World:

I don’t usually read the same author back to back. But there’s nothing usual about Murakami. “Fairness is a concept that holds only in limited situations. Yet we want the concept to extend to everything, in and out of phase. From snails to hardware stores to married life. Maybe no one finds it, or even misses it, but fairness is like love. What is given has nothing to do with what we seek.”

My Enemy, Myself:

Yuram Binur is an Israeli journalist that is commonly mistaken for an Arab during his work assignments. Binur decides to test the limits of this assumption by living as a Palestinian laborer. Binur’s firsthand accounts reveal the treatment of Palestinian workers, the difficulty of just trying to live under constant occupation (such as attempting to meet a friend at the airport), what it is like for a Palestinian to be in a relationship with an Israeli, and how even passive individuals can become “radicalized” as a result of Israeli policies and more. Additionally, this experiment allows Binur to reflect on his own past behavior and treatment of Palestinians.The parallels between “Black Like Me” are quite obvious. But Binur’s details of labor and search for employment kept reminding me of George Orwell’s accounts in “Down and Out in Paris and London”. Finally, don’t let the age of this book fool you. It is every bit as relevant as when it was published over two decades ago. “If there is anything extraordinary about what is happening today, it is only that it hasn’t happened sooner.”

Gulp. Adventures on the Alimentary Canal:

Did you know humans have two kinds of spit? What about how the ability to chew is so precious that patients will choose being mute over the inability to swallow? Why do drug mules prefer the danger of swallowing their parcel over the use of their other body cavity? Finally, does our aversion and taboo surrounding words like "rectum" delay medical progress? Mary Roach's work addresses these questions and many more. An informational book that is just as captivating as a novel. Mary Roach just might be the the most charismatic science writer. "People like what they eat, rather than eat what they like." -Mary Roach

The Home and the World:

A woman named Bimala is in the peculiar position of having a life outside pudrah. Her husband Nikhil supports her independence and curiosity but this liberty leads to a questionable relationship with Nikhil's friend Sandip. Her autonomy does not come without struggles. Bimala finds herself entwined between Nikhil and Sandip. Nikhil's pragmatic views and pacifism cannot coexist with Sandip's idealism and thirst for India's independence regardless of the cost. The freedom of self-determination that allows her to be an individual is also the same freedom that is having her choose between her home and her world. Tagore's allegory for India's independence movement is 100 years old yet it is not any less germane. Sandip's indifference towards violence and his disingenuous beliefs will remind you of today's demagogues and the romanticization of nationalism. Nikhil's patience and limitless clemency may be honorable yet it still deserves criticism. He may not support Sandip's extremism but he does defend a reprehensible status quo. In the end, Nikhil and Sandip share the same fault. They both lack what their counterpart has. Nikhil is so obsessed with taking the "right" action that if he ever acts it is too late. Whereas, Sandip's arrogance leads to actions regardless of right and wrong. If either character had an iota of their counterpart then it would be possible for Bimala to make a decision she can be happy with. Instead she is left with a lose-lose situation. Everyone deserves a home and everyone deserves the world. No one should have to choose. "Because you have lighted a fire, it does not follow that you have the food to cook over it."

The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time:

Christoper (a 15 year old autistic savant) discovers that the neighbor's dog has been murdered. Can he solve the case while maintaining his routine? How long will he be able to avoid the color yellow? Will it be a "Super Good Day" or a "Black Day"? "Sometimes people want to be stupid and they do not want to know the truth." -Christopher John Francis Boone

Snow:

After a dozen years in exile a poet named Ka has returned to Turkey. The Institute of Education in Kars has implemented a prohibition on women wearing head scarves. This act has lead to a series of suicides that Ka has come to investigate. Ka might be in Kars to report on the events but he also has his own reasons for returning. What follows is a story with multiple narrators that might be opaque but it remains captivating. Orhan Pamuk is telling a story of a person, a city, and a nation. Pamuk's ability to frequently use foreshadowing while still including twists and surprises is impressive and humbling. One might become annoyed at the narrator(s) for "spoilers" but one would be mistaken to call them that. "People don't know when they're happy, at least not at the moment." -Ka

Rules for Radicals:

Reading this was long overdue. "The life of man upon earth is a warfare..." -Job 7:1

Seven Pillars of Wisdom

"All men dream, but not equally." Seven Pillars of Wisdom: An autobiographical account that resembles a Greek epic. T.E. Lawrence takes you with him during his firsthand account of the Arab Revolt. There was more to the Great War than a Western Front and Lawrence illustrates it.

Exposure:

“The suspicion that anxiety isn’t ‘real’ is especially tormenting because not knowing what’s real is perhaps the worst part.”

Nausea:

A story about an adventurer/writer named Antoine. Antoine is living in Bouville (Mud town) when he starts to get the impression that experiences and objects interfere with his existence and understanding himself. "I exist that's all. And that trouble is so vague, so metaphysical that I am ashamed of it." -Antoine Roquentin

War and the Iliad:

Simone Weil and Rachel Bespaloff were both WWII refugees and they both turned to Homer's "Iliad" for answers. Each of them provide fascinating ideas and insight that will challenge you to reexamine the "Iliad" or compel you to pick it it up for the very first time. "Force is as pitiless to the man who possesses it, or thinks he does, as it is to its victims; the second it crushes, the first it intoxicates. The truth is, nobody really possesses it." -Simone Weil "Who is good in the Iliad? Who is bad? Such distinctions do not exist; there are only men suffering, warriors fighting, some winning, some losing." -Rachel Bespaloff

Catch a Fire:

The story of Bob Marley but also the story of Jamaica and Rastafari. "Free speech carries with it some freedom to listen." I cannot say enough good things about this masterpiece. Bob Marley wasn't the first musician I ever heard but he was the first musician I became obsessed with. I was in grade school when I tagged along with my Dad to a record shop called Vintage Vinyl. I ended up leaving with a used copy of "Legend". I didn't have an iPod but I did have an MP3 player that was big enough to store the album and that's as big as it needed to be. For the rest of grade school if I didn't have to be in uniform I'd be in my Bob Marley shirt just in case there was a punky reggae party.

Cosmicomics:

The ingenious creativity and complexity yet simplicity of this book lead me to ask "What the hell just happened?" and immediately reread it in an attempt to understand. A collection of 12 short stories involving evolution, math, space, time and most importantly love. Italo Calvino takes mathematical and scientific concepts and morphs them into captivating narratives. I have not read a work that so eloquently romanticizes math and science since Abbott's "Flatland". "When you've waited two hundred million years, you can also wait six hundred." -Qfwfq

White Noise:

  • Jack- the founder of Hitler studies but can't speak German
  • Babette- reads tabloids to the blind and teaches posture to the elderly
  • Murray- finds the meaning in everything
  • Steffie- has a compulsion to burn toast
  • Heinrich- is only happy during a disaster
  • Wilder- Where is Wilder?

A story that keeps you interested because of the dialog. Never mind the plot. "A person spends his life saying good-bye to other people. How does he say good-bye to himself?"
